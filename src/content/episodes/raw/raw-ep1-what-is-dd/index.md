New to D&D? Not sure where to start? Naked and afraid? We can help (with one of those).

This episode gives a quick introduction to D&D 5th Edition, covering the absolute basics for anyone curious about how to play, including dice, character sheets, and the general flow of a session. It’s perfect if you only have a short time to explain the game.

## **Dice and Basic Mechanics**

D&D uses a set of 7 polyhedral dice, each denoted by a "D" followed by its number of sides (e.g., 1d6 for one 6-sided die, 1d20 for one 20-sided die, which you'll roll often). The game's core involves dice rolls for skill checks (DM asks you to roll a skill, like Animal Handling, against a Difficulty Class), saving throws (resisting danger, related to your 6 main stats), and attack rolls (hitting an enemy's Armor Class, often with proficiency bonus added) and damage rolls. Proficiency adds a bonus to certain rolls, increasing with your character's level. The guys discuss a variant where proficiency is a dice roll instead of a static number, which adds more randomness but can be fun. Critical successes (natural 20) and failures (natural 1) are a common house rule for skill checks, but rules as written, they only apply to attack rolls. Be sure to establish expectations for these with your DM in Session Zero.

## **Character Sheets**

First impressions of a character sheet can be intimidating due to all the numbers and blank spaces. The left side typically holds your main stats (Strength, Dexterity, Constitution, Intelligence, Wisdom, Charisma), skills, and saving throws – the "meat and potatoes" of gameplay. The right side has more character-building elements like ideals, bonds, and flaws. These can be pre-filled or developed through play, depending on your campaign and DM's style. Sometimes they become less relevant as your character grows naturally.

## **Digital vs. Paper**

There's a big debate on digital vs. paper character sheets. Digital sheets (on phones or laptops) offer convenience for tracking extensive inventories, spell lists, or vehicle stats, especially at higher levels where abilities accumulate. However, DMs might find them distracting, creating physical barriers, or encouraging players to disengage. A good middle ground can be using small, personal paper character sheet booklets for day-to-day main stats and abilities, while relying on digital tools for complex accounting like spellbooks and inventories, which are referred to less frequently.

## **Game Flow**

Gameplay generally splits into non-combat and combat sections. Non-combat focuses on social interactions, problem-solving, and team objectives, allowing for creative use of abilities. Combat is more rules-heavy, centered on stats, HP, and making numbers hit zero. D&D is ultimately a collaborative storytelling experience, with the DM establishing the environment and players shaping the narrative. While combat is well-fleshed out in the rules, role-playing, often driven by skill checks, is where the most memorable moments happen.
